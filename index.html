1 Lesson Overview

Aims & Competence Goals

By the end of this lesson you will be able to read and summarise an English-language source, and create your own professional food portrait with a written description.

🎯 Lesson Aim

To explore how diet varies across different cultures and countries, using the photo project "Daily Bread" by Gregg Segal as a source. You will analyse what people eat, compare it to your own diet, and produce a Canva image with a short written text documenting your own weekly food intake — skills directly relevant to your studies in restaurant and food processing.

7 Writing Task

Write About Your Own Diet

Write a short text (120–180 words) describing what you eat in a typical week. This text will be sent to Barbara together with your Canva image. Choose the frame that fits your level.

🎯
Remember: Your audience is your teacher Barbara. Write in a clear, structured way. Describe what you eat, reflect on whether it is healthy or not, and connect it to what you learned from the photo project about global diets.
🟢 Support Frame
🟡 Guided Frame
🔵 Free Writing

Use this frame if you need a lot of support. Fill in the blanks with your own information.

My Weekly Diet

My name is your name and I am a student at school name. I study restaurant and food processing.

During a typical week, I usually eat 2–3 foods for breakfast. For lunch, I often have food. In the evenings, my family usually cooks meal.

My favourite food is food. I eat it about once / twice / three times a week.

Compared to the kids in the photo project, my diet is similar to / different from the children in country. This is because reason.

I think my diet is healthy / quite healthy / could be healthier because reason.
💡 Tip: Replace all the underlined sections with your own words. Try to write in full sentences.

Use this frame if you can write some sentences on your own but need a structure to follow.

Paragraph 1 — Introduction
Introduce yourself briefly. Mention that you are going to describe your typical weekly diet.
Useful phrases: "In this text, I will describe…" / "My weekly diet consists of…"

Paragraph 2 — What I eat
Describe your meals during the week: breakfast, lunch, dinner and snacks. Give specific examples.
Useful phrases: "For breakfast, I usually…" / "I tend to eat… for lunch" / "A typical dinner in my home is…"

Paragraph 3 — Comparison with the photo project
Compare your diet to one of the countries from the photo project. What is similar? What is different?
Useful phrases: "Compared to…" / "Unlike the children in…" / "Similar to…, I also…"

Paragraph 4 — Reflection
Reflect: Is your diet healthy? What would you like to change? Why is diet important for someone in the food industry?
Useful phrases: "I think my diet is…" / "I could improve my diet by…" / "As someone studying food, I believe…"

Write your own text without a template. Use these prompts to guide your thinking.

Think about:
• What does your diet say about your culture and background?
• How does what you eat compare to global patterns shown in the photo project?
• Are processed foods a big part of your diet? Does this connect to anything from the photos?
• As a future professional in the food industry, what role do you think you have in helping people eat better?
• What surprised you most from the photo project?

Language challenges to try:
• Use contrast language: "However…", "On the other hand…", "In contrast to…"
• Use hedging language: "I tend to…", "It seems that…", "In general…"
• Use formal vocabulary from the vocabulary section above

💬 Useful Sentence Starters

Describing what you eat ▼
  • I usually eat / have / drink… for breakfast / lunch / dinner.
  • A typical meal in my household includes…
  • I tend to eat a lot of… / very little…
  • My diet mainly consists of…
  • On weekdays, I often…, but on weekends I…
Comparing with the photo project ▼
  • Compared to the children in [country], my diet is…
  • Unlike [country], where people eat mainly…, I tend to…
  • Similar to many kids in [country], I also eat a lot of…
  • One thing that surprised me in the photo project was that…
  • The biggest difference between my diet and [country]'s diet is…
Reflecting on health and the food industry ▼
  • I think my diet is fairly healthy / could be healthier because…
  • I could improve my diet by eating more… / less…
  • As a student of restaurant and food processing, I think it is important to…
  • The photo project made me reflect on…
  • Understanding different food cultures is relevant to my studies because…
8 Creative Task

Create Your Food Portrait

Inspired by Gregg Segal's photos, you will now create your own version using Canva. Place a photo of yourself in the centre and arrange images of your weekly foods around you.

🎨 Your Canva Food Portrait

Follow these steps carefully. You have approximately 25 minutes for this task.

1
Open Canva and start a new design

Go to canva.com and log in. Click "Create a design" and choose A4 Document or Instagram Post (Square) as your format.

2
Upload your photo

Click "Uploads" on the left panel → "Upload files" → choose a photo of yourself. Drag it to the centre of the canvas and resize it so it takes up about 30–40% of the space.

3
Add food images around you

Use Canva's search to find images of the foods you eat during the week. Type the food name in the search bar → choose "Elements" or "Photos" → drag the image onto your canvas. Arrange food images around your photo. Aim for at least 8–10 different foods.

4
Add your name and a title

Use the "Text" tool to add your name and a title such as "My Week in Food" or "What I Eat in a Week". Choose a font and colour that looks good with your design.

5
Adjust and style your design

Choose a background colour or texture. Make sure everything is readable and well-arranged. Use the alignment tools to keep things tidy.

6
Download your image

Click "Share" (top right) → "Download" → choose PNG or PDF format → click "Download". Save the file to send to Barbara.

🎨 Open Canva — canva.com
💡
Design tip: Look at Gregg Segal's photographs for inspiration. Notice how he arranges food in a circle or spread around the person. Try to recreate that feeling — you are the centre of your own food story!
